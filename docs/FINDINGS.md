# Findings and results

This document records the headline outcomes of the master evaluation. The numbers come from the 24-month run on the locked 2024-01-01 to 2025-12-31 test split, generated by `scripts/master_eval.py` and stored at `output/master_eval/run_24month/master_report.txt` together with the CSV suite alongside it. Re-running the same script with the same checkpoints reproduces every number on this page byte for byte.

Five hero figures from the 24-month run sit under `../assets/results/` and are embedded inline below at the natural boundaries (leaderboard, DM panel, transfer matrices, per-pair winners). Each PNG is rendered at 600 DPI from the master-eval CSV outputs.

## Executive summary

- The platform itself reproduces. Same code, same seeds, same checkpoints, byte-identical `results_all.csv` across re-runs. RQ0 is answered.
- Three configurations earn an A or B grade on the 24-month test window. The remaining 578 evaluated configurations sit at C or below.
- One single rule-based configuration cleared the T1-to-T4 selection-and-stability filters: USDCAD with `MACD_f26_s65_sig9`, all-session, long-short. Six of seven pairs lost their T3 winner to the T4 negative-fold filter.
- No Diebold-Mariano test of best-strategy versus buy-and-hold reaches `p < 0.05` for either rule-based or ML strategies. The point-estimate winners are real Sharpe positives, but the trade counts on the winning configurations are too small to reject the null hypothesis under DM.
- Two of fourteen in-domain-versus-transfer DM probes are significant (USDCHF London at `p = 0.0399`, NZDUSD Asia at `p = 0.0329`). That figure approximates the false-positive rate expected under the null at `alpha = 0.05`. Session conditioning is therefore not a reliable effect across pairs, even though particular cells (notably USDCHF LSTM_ny -> asia at `+2.83` Sharpe) show real edges.

## Verdicts on the three research questions

### RQ0 reproducibility

Answered yes. Same pipeline, same seeds, same locked splits, byte-identical `results_all.csv` across re-runs. The operational test:

```bash
mv output/master_eval/run_24month/results_all.csv \
   output/master_eval/run_24month/results_all_run1.csv
python scripts/master_eval.py --spreads 1.0
diff output/master_eval/run_24month/results_all_run1.csv \
     output/master_eval/run_24month/results_all.csv
```

An empty diff is the verdict. The pytest suite (twelve files, sixty-three tests) covers the regressions that erode reproducibility silently: wrong index alignment between features and labels, scaler fit on the wrong split, session masks that include or exclude the boundary minute inconsistently, fold-path resolution drifting between row-index slicing and on-disk Parquet reads.

### RQ1 session conditioning

Answered: mixed and pair-specific. No uniform claim of the form "session conditioning helps" survives the data.

The session-generalisability table shows that Asia-trained models transfer the worst on average (`avg off-diagonal Sharpe = -8.12`) and London-trained models transfer the best (`-5.67`). The DM test for in-domain versus transfer is significant on only two of fourteen pair-by-session probes (USDCHF London at `p = 0.0399`, NZDUSD Asia at `p = 0.0329`). For the remaining twelve probes, in-session training is statistically indistinguishable from training on a different session.

A handful of session-by-eval cells do produce profitable transfer, and they cluster around the same intuition: train on the session with the sharpest regime signal and evaluate on a different session. The notable cells are EURUSD `LR_asia` evaluated globally (Sharpe `+2.71`), USDCAD `LSTM_ny` evaluated on Asia (Sharpe `+3.96`), and USDCHF `LSTM_ny` evaluated on Asia (Sharpe `+2.83`). The pattern suggests that session conditioning matters when the value is in cross-session generalisation rather than in-domain prediction.

### RQ2 model complexity (LSTM versus LR)

Answered: no on aggregate. The two architectures produce roughly equivalent leaderboard tops, with each architecture winning on different pairs.

Family-level averages on the test window:

- `ml_lr`:   -7.90 net Sharpe
- `ml_lstm`: -6.43 net Sharpe

Both are strongly negative. The architecture difference (`-7.90` versus `-6.43`) is small relative to the variance across pairs and sessions. The DM `ML_champion_vs_runner_up` test never reaches significance for any pair (all `p > 0.19`), so the apparent LR-versus-LSTM ranking flips at the top of the leaderboard cannot be claimed as a real edge.

Reading the unified leaderboard top three: the rank-one cell is LSTM, rank two is LR, and rank three is rule-based. Three different model classes for the top three slots. The honest interpretation: high-quality features and validation discipline matter more than architecture complexity at one-minute FX resolution.

## What worked exceptionally well

<details>
<summary>USDCHF LSTM_ny -> asia (composite 84.7, grade A, Sharpe +2.83)</summary>

The single A-grade cell in the run. The model is a multi-scale LSTM trained on USDCHF NY-session data and evaluated on USDCHF Asia-session bars. The cell shows a 32-trade count (low; not enough for DM significance), a maximum drawdown of 0.45 percent, and a Sortino of 7.70.

The cell is a cross-session-transfer success: the NY session is where USDCHF carries most of its directional volatility, and projecting that learned representation onto the quieter Asia session produces a strategy that takes few trades but loses very little when those trades go wrong. Reproduce with:

```bash
python backtest/run_backtest.py --pair USDCHF --strategy LSTM_ny \
  --split test --from 2024-01-01 --to 2025-12-31 \
  --capital 10000 --spread 1.0 --no-browser
```

</details>

<details>
<summary>EURUSD LR_asia -> global (composite 77.2, grade B, Sharpe +2.71)</summary>

A Logistic Regression trained on EURUSD Asia-session bars and evaluated against the full global test split. The cell carries 267 trades, a 0.74 percent maximum drawdown, and a Calmar of 3.64. The same training run also produced the rank-four cell (`LR_asia` evaluated on NY, Sharpe `+2.51`) and the rank-five cell (`LR_asia` evaluated on London, Sharpe `+2.23`). Three of the top five leaderboard slots therefore come from the same underlying checkpoint; the strategy generalises well across the eval-session axis once trained on Asia data.

</details>

<details>
<summary>USDCAD MACD_f26_s65_sig9 (composite 76.0, grade B, Sharpe +2.51)</summary>

The only rule-based configuration in the entire universe that cleared the T1-to-T4 selection and stability filters. The strategy is a long-short MACD on USDCAD with a 26-period fast EMA, a 65-period slow EMA, and a 9-period signal line. It carries 23 trades over the 24-month window, a 0.75 percent maximum drawdown, and a Sortino of 7.78.

The cell exists because the T4 negative-fold filter is doing real work. Six of seven pairs had their T3 winners killed at T4 by the `negative_fold_count > 2` filter; only USDCAD MACD survived. The configuration is therefore evidence that the tiered pipeline does isolate genuinely stable rule-based strategies, even when only one survives the gate.

</details>

## What did not work, and why

The platform's negative findings are worth reporting in the same spirit as the positive ones. Five categories.

**Most strategies.** Of 581 evaluated configurations, 528 grade D or F (composite below 40). The leaderboard is heavily right-skewed; only a small handful of cells produce trade-able edges. The 0-to-100 composite score with the cost gate (`n_trades < 10` or `max_drawdown < -0.95` zeros the score) is a strict filter, and that strictness is the point.

**Cross-session transfer.** Average off-diagonal Sharpe runs from -5.7 (London-trained, the best) down to -8.1 (Asia-trained, the worst). Models trained in one session usually lose money when evaluated on another. The pattern matches the FX practitioner intuition: each session has its own dominant order-flow regime, and a model fit to one session can pick up patterns that simply do not generalise to the others.

**Deep momentum strategies.** `Momentum_lb120` is the worst rule-based strategy on six of seven pairs (Sharpe -4 to -8 in those cells). The combination of long lookback, minute-level noise, and per-trade spread cost is consistent loss. The strategy survives at the leaderboard's middle only because USDJPY's 24-month uptrend was strong enough to carry the lookback through the cost gate; on every other pair, the momentum signal is overwhelmed by spread.

**NZDUSD across the board.** NZDUSD is the worst pair in five of six strategy families. The 1.4-pip spread is the widest in the universe; the intraday range is the narrowest among the seven majors during the test window. The combination eats almost any signal a strategy can produce.

**High AUC, low net Sharpe.** Several LR and LSTM cells classify directional movement above chance but lose money after spread. The mismatch is the classical "high AUC trap" the project's evaluation principles are written to defend against: classification metrics are diagnostic only; economic metrics are the ranking key.
